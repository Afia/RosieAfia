Is doing something better than nothing?

Paying to volunteer.

You might have heard people say, "doing something is better than doing nothing". But it isn't. Not when that something is often wasteful at best, and at worst causing a lot of harm.

The growing practice of young people going abroad to volunteer (voluntourism) is because hundreds of thousands of young adventurers go abroad to volunteer each year, as part of school requirements, to build their CVs, as part of gap year trips, or as part of a medical or vocational degree. It might make people feel good about themselves - but it can be detrimental to those who are supposed to be helped. Much of this demand is fuelled by the belief that because we come from financially wealthier countries, we have the right and obligation, to bestow our benevolence on people. Never mind if we don't speak the language, don't have the skills or experience to qualify for the jobs we're doing, or don't know anything about what life is like "over there".

Voluntourism can be great.

Having managed a volnteer program, I am in no way attempting to criticise the good intentions of volunteer travellers. It is a fantastic way to travel and to get to know a community and society. I know from my own experience that our desire to help is sincere - but I now also know that good intentions are not enough.

We need to focus on learning first - not just encouraging jumping in. Like the business intern serving coffee and learning what it takes to be a good lawyer, their most significant impact in the role is not achieved in a short time, but rather in avoiding being too much of a distraction in the short-term and learning how to have a real impact in the long run.

Paying to volunteer can be an inexpensive, fulfilling and rewarding experience. However, you need to ensure that you choose a good company and more importantly, a sustainable project.
